Most travel planning does not begin with a clean itinerary. It begins with a mess.

There is a flight confirmation in email. A hotel you might book. A restaurant someone mentioned. A PDF from a ferry company. Three links from a friend. A note that says "slow morning after arrival" because you know yourself. A museum ticket, a half-decided day trip, a family request, a place you definitely want to avoid, and a budget conversation that is still unresolved.

That mess is not a failure of planning. It is what real planning looks like.

The problem is that most tools pretend the mess does not exist. A chat thread gives you ideas but hides the source material. A document holds information but becomes hard to use on the road. A spreadsheet tracks decisions but does not feel like a trip. Notes apps collect fragments, then make you search through them while you are standing outside a station.

OurTrips is built around a simpler belief: the messy pile is valuable, but the traveler should not have to carry it as a messy pile.

## Collect first

The first power of OurTrips is information collection.

You can start a trip inside OurTrips now. You do not need to begin in Claude, Cowork, Codex, or any other external agent. The app asks the travel-agent basics: where you are going, when, who is traveling, where you start from, how you like to move, what is booked, what matters, and what still feels uncertain.

Then you can add the real material. Paste notes. Add known bookings. Upload references. Mention preferences. Bring the things that normally live across email, maps, messages, bookmarks, and memory.

This matters because travel planning is rarely only about "what are the top ten things to do?" It is about combining public information with your constraints. Arrival time. Hotel area. Energy level. Budget. Reservation status. Weather risk. How much your group wants to walk. The day after a long transfer.

A good plan needs those details.

## Plan for anticipation

Trip planning is also part of the trip.

There is a specific kind of fun in seeing the journey take shape before you leave. You imagine a morning. You debate a restaurant. You move one activity because a neighborhood suddenly makes sense. You show the plan to someone else and feel the trip become more real.

OurTrips should support that anticipation instead of reducing planning to output generation.

The built-in travel agent creates a first draft from your material, but the important part is what happens next. You can keep editing the real itinerary. Ask for a lighter day. Mark a hotel as booked. Add a dinner reservation. Replace a museum. Keep maps, stays, meals, route points, and practical notes connected.

The chat is not a separate document. It is attached to the trip.

## Carry the day

The third power is on-the-go access.

When you are traveling, the most important interface is the day you are in. You should not have to search through the whole plan to answer simple questions:

- What are we doing this morning?
- Where are we sleeping tonight?
- What is booked?
- What still needs a decision?
- How do we get from here to there?
- Where was that lunch place?

OurTrips organizes the guide day by day. Each day can carry the plan, map, stay, meals, transport, reservation notes, and practical tips. Trips can be shared and saved for offline access, because the moment you need the plan is often the moment your connection is least cooperative.

That is the real product: not just planning a trip, but turning the planning mess into a guide that still makes sense when the trip starts.

## The new starting point

This is the biggest shift in OurTrips so far.

OurTrips used to be mostly a destination for work you did somewhere else. Plan in an external agent, then send the finished itinerary to OurTrips. That still works if you want it, through the connector.

But the primary flow is now different. Start in OurTrips. Collect the material. Let the agent create the trip. Keep refining the actual guide. Take it with you.

Travel planning is messy. OurTrips does not pretend otherwise. It gives the mess somewhere useful to go.
